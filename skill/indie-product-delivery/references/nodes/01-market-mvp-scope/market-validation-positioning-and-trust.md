# Market Validation, Positioning, and Trust

Use this guide when demand, alternatives, pricing, positioning, distribution, trust, or the public visual world needs evidence before a product decision.

## Validation, Positioning, and Visual World

#### 1. Validate demand and wedge

1. Use web/search by default for demand, competitors, pricing, commercial scale, and public market reality unless the user opts out or the idea is confidential.
2. Search user pain, alternatives including current workaround, commercialization, wedge space, and false-positive risk.
3. Prefer behavior-rich sources: pricing pages, reviews, support/community threads, issue trackers, case studies, job posts, and company signals.
4. Classify evidence by behavior, money, workaround, cost, urgency, and switching rather than compliments.
5. Assess whether the wedge is narrow, painful, reachable, and difficult for incumbents to prioritize.

Treat the status quo as the primary competitor. Map the current sequence, people, tools, handoffs, delay,
money, mistakes, anxiety, and reason the user tolerates it. Include spreadsheets, messages, manual labor,
internal tools, avoidance, and doing nothing. A competitor feature grid is secondary to understanding why the
user has not switched already.

| Strength | Evidence |
|---:|---|
| 1 | payment, renewal, pre-order, signed pilot, budget |
| 2 | repeated workaround, support issue, churn complaint, painful migration |
| 3 | observed workflow or past-behavior interview |
| 4 | competitor traction, pricing, hiring, logos, category growth |
| 5 | landing conversion, demo request, waitlist, signup |
| 6 | survey preference, compliments, likes |

#### Evidence interpretation

1. Prefer the user's own words for the outcome over the founder's category language. Preserve short source phrases when they reveal a different job, urgency, or value proposition.
2. Distinguish acquisition curiosity, activation, repeated use, retention, payment, expansion, and distress at loss. Do not use an earlier-stage signal as proof of a later-stage behavior.
3. Prefer observation without guidance over a polished demo. Record where the user hesitates, invents a
   workaround, misunderstands the promise, or uses the product for an unplanned job.
4. Treat surprise as information. If every observation matches the plan, check whether the founder guided the session, selected friendly users, or ignored behavior that did not fit the narrative.
5. State the strongest evidence against the direction and what result would reverse the current verdict.

When landscape research matters, synthesize three layers instead of copying competitor conventions:

| Layer | Question |
|---|---|
| established pattern | What reliably works in this category, and why? |
| current evidence | What are products, users, pricing, reviews, and failures showing now? |
| first principles | Where might convention be solving an old constraint or serving a different segment? |

Use disagreement between layers to form a testable differentiation premise, not an automatic contrarian
feature. If the conventional pattern is strongly supported, raise the evidence bar before departing from it.

#### 2. Check bootstrapped viability

For a commercial or sustained product, test more than demand:

1. Identify who uses, who pays, who can block adoption, and how the founder can reach the first ten credible users without assuming a future channel.
2. Check willingness to pay or another concrete exchange, the switching trigger, sales/onboarding friction,
   support burden, recurring manual labor, provider/content cost, and likely gross-margin pressure.
3. Prefer a narrow reachable segment with urgent pain over a larger abstract market. Mark assumptions when
   pricing, acquisition, retention, or service cost is not yet known.
4. State the next deadline by which the product must produce learning, active use, or revenue to justify more
   founder time.

#### Distribution and adoption path

1. Name the first reachable channel and why the target user is present there. Avoid placeholders such as
   "content", "community", "SEO", or "partnerships" without a concrete audience and action.
2. Describe how the user receives the result: web flow, email, shared artifact, package, marketplace, app
   store, integration, human delivery, or another real channel.
3. Identify setup cost, trust gate, procurement or sponsor gate, switching cost, and who owns adoption after the first demo.
4. For internal products, test whether the product survives a sponsor change; for external products, test
   whether acquisition and onboarding can work without founder explanation every time.
5. Explicitly defer distribution infrastructure to Node02/06 when appropriate, but never defer the product
   decision of how value reaches the first user.

#### 3. Choose validation with a decision rule

Use the lightest method that tests the riskiest assumption. Interviews test pain/workaround; concierge or
Wizard-of-Oz tests value before automation; fake-door/CTA and landing smoke test intent/positioning; prototype
observation tests comprehension/trust; paid pilots test budget/adoption; public source mining tests repeated
pain. Every method needs a segment, activation event, success metric, guardrail, kill/pivot signal, and time
box.

Turn the choice into one concrete evidence action:

```text
Riskiest assumption: <what must be true>
Current evidence: <observed / inferred / absent>
Cheapest next action: <one real-world test>
Success threshold: <observable result and time box>
Failure threshold: <kill, narrow, reframe, or research signal>
Decision after result: <build, revise, defer, or stop>
```

The action must test behavior, money, workaround, comprehension, or repeated pain. Avoid assignments that
only produce more opinions or a longer document. If the founder chooses to build before evidence, record the
Must assumption and the earliest product event that can test it.

Choose observation tasks that can contradict the plan:

- watch a target user attempt the workflow without instruction;
- ask for a paid pilot, pre-order, or concrete internal commitment;
- deliver the outcome manually and measure whether the user returns;
- expose a narrow fake door or CTA with an honest follow-up;
- interview about the last real occurrence, current workaround, and cost rather than future preference;
- mine support, review, issue, or community evidence for repeated behavior and language.

For future fit, state one specific external change over the next one to three years, the mechanism by which it
changes the user's job, and whether that makes this product more essential, replaceable, or obsolete. Market
growth and generic technology improvement are context, not a product thesis.

Use one verdict: `Validated enough for MVP/MAP`, `Promising but unvalidated`, `Interesting but weak`,
`Likely vitamin`, or `Blocked by evidence gap`.

#### 4. Define positioning and public trust

For a landing, pricing, brand, trust, portfolio, or other public surface:

1. Name its business job: conversion, activation, trust, pricing clarity, onboarding, retention, or
   credibility.
2. Separate audience, buyer, and user. State the promise as progress created, pain reduced, and why now.
3. Capture trust objections: price, privacy, security, reliability, migration, proof, support, or legitimacy.
4. Choose a metaphor/positioning anchor only when it clarifies the product.
5. Define Node04 constraints: tone, density, motion appetite, proof assets, image direction, copy voice,
   credibility signals, and non-goals.

| Surface signal | Required handoff |
|---|---|
| new landing/public page | promise, audience, proof, trust objections, visual-world brief |
| generic or untrusted redesign | positioning gap and preservation constraints |
| pricing/conversion | buyer anxiety, value proof, packaging promise |
| brand/logo | category, audience, metaphor, visual world, misuse boundaries |
| "make it premium" | what premium means for this segment and decision |
